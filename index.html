1.1 Why Young Adults + Wellness + Dynamic Pricing

Target Insurees

Young adults drop out


U.S. Census Bureau (2019) report average uninsured rate:

  • adults aged 19–34: 15.6%

  • adults aged 35–64: 11.3%


Covered California’s 2014 launch shows 28% of enrollees were aged 18–34, even though they made up 33% of the state’s population.

Lifestyle awareness

Chronic Diseases & Lifestyle


90% of U.S. health expenditures are linked to chronic diseases

70–80% of chronic conditions are preventable through lifestyle changes such as healthy diet, exercise, and avoiding smoking.

60%+ of U.S. adults underestimate their own lifestyle-related health risks.

Dynamic Pricing

Engagement Gap


Wellness programs (e.g., Attain by Aetna, UHC Motion) show short-term engagement spikes but long-term drop-offs, often shutting down or scaling back.

Current rewards (gift cards, discounts, wearables) are decoupled from actual premiums, so users lack strong continuous motivation.

1.2.1 Problem for Existing Solutions

1.2.1 Problem for Existing Solutions

❌ User segmentation & fairness

Current models treat all users similarly, without accounting for differences in age, baseline health, lifestyle, or risk profile.

❌ User growth & acquisition

Fail to drive sustained user growth and new user acquisition. Only engage existing policyholders on short-term retention, but fail to drive sustained growth.

Extra cost for Incentives

Most wellness programs has cashback, gift cards, or wearable discounts, instead of implementing genuine dynamic premium adjustments.

How might we motivate young adults to lead healthier lives in a way that benefits both them and their insurance providers?

2.1.2.1 ⭐ Key Takeaways

Dynamic Pricing & Fairness

Overall receptivity to dynamic pricing is high, but fairness safeguards are needed to boost GMV and sustain long-term user trust and retention (maintain and improve NPS).

Impact of Health Metrics & Sense of Achievement

Clearly articulate the value and impact of health data, improve measurement accuracy, and cultivate a sense of achievement to more effectively drive behavior change.

Actionable Privacy, Trust & Transparency

Be transparent about data sharing, explain the reasons behind premium changes, and offer an appeal process—without disrupting the business—to strengthen retention and NPS.

04 - Challenges

4.1.1 Trust Crisis

4.1.1 Trust Crisis

Adversarial System

The inherent opposition of the system makes user feel insurers profit when they lose. Insurance feels like a zero-sum game

Opaque Scoring

Premium changes and “health scores” are black boxes; no one knows what behaviors actually matter.

Data Commodification

“I need data & number so I can make decision and action (to choose dynamic Pricing and improve wellness).”

4.1.2 Our Design Strategy

4.1.2 Our Design Strategy

4.2.1 Ethical Risk & Data Privacy

4.2.1 Ethical Risk & Data Privacy

Proxy Bias

The danger that the model mistakes "Access to Resources" for "Personal Responsibility," unfairly penalizing those with fewer economic means.

Systemic Exclusion

Algorithm might "cherry-picks" the healthy, effectively barring those with chronic conditions or disabilities from affordable access.

Mental Anxiety

The pressure to maintain perfect stats creates mental stress, turning wellness into an obsessive, high-stakes chore.

5.1 Business and Revenue Model

5.1 Business and Revenue Model

Customer

Young, health-conscious consumers purchasing insurance directly through the platform.

User quote

Primarily consumers through monthly insurance premiums; wellness brands contribute via ads or affiliates.

Pricing Model

Base Premium — Flat monthly insurance fee.

  • Dynamic Adjustments — 5–15% discounts tied to verified health behaviors (activity, sleep, routines).

  • Add-Ons — Optional paid wellness services and programs.

  • Supplementary Revenue — Lightweight CPM/CPC/CPA ads + affiliate fees in the wellness marketplace.

Our position (B2C):

Wellness apps & incentives

Attain, Lark

Track activity and offer gift cards / credits; more personalized coaching, but weak link to core pricing.

Behavior-linked insurance leaders

Oscar, John Hancock + Vitality, Betterfly

Start connecting lifestyle to benefits/premium discounts, but often complex, employer-only, or partial.

5.5 Value Proposition

5.5 Value Proposition

Fair, dynamic premiums & gentle motivation

Transparent, personalized pricing with small, understandable adjustments that nudge healthier habits—not punish lapses or over-monitor.

Clear, user-controlled data & agency

Users see what’s tracked, how it affects cost, and can change sharing anytime, reducing data anxiety and “black box” feeling—especially for first-time insured students.

Win-win economics for users and insurers

Users get tangible financial value from their data; insurers gain better risk signals, lower long-term risk, and early relationships with young adults.
